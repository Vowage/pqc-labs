Concept Capsule: Why the Math Breaks

No quantum physics required. No proofs. Just the intuition you need to understand what's at stake and why NIST's new algorithms are different.

Why RSA Works — and Why That's a Problem

RSA's security rests on a single observation: multiplying two large prime numbers is trivially easy, but finding the original primes from their product is computationally impossible at scale. This asymmetry between "easy to make, impossible to reverse" is the foundation of public-key cryptography.

The Easy Direction
P × Q

Takes milliseconds
↔

The Hard Direction
N = ?
Given only the product N, find P and Q.
RSA-2048: classical computer takes longer than the universe's age
🔑 Analogy: The Phone Book Finding a person's phone number in a phone book given their name is easy. Finding a name given only a phone number (with no index) requires checking every single entry. RSA relies on this kind of one-way difficulty. The question quantum computing poses is: what if you could check all entries simultaneously?

Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC) Has the Same Problem

ECC uses a different mathematical structure — points on a curve — but its security relies on the same class of problem: the discrete logarithm problem. Given a point on a curve, finding the scalar that produced it is computationally infeasible classically. Shor's algorithm solves this efficiently too.

ECC is actually more efficient than RSA (smaller keys for equivalent security), which is why it's in everything from HTTPS to SSH. But it shares RSA's quantum vulnerability.

Shor's Algorithm: Why Quantum Changes Everything

Shor's algorithm (1994) is not just a "faster" factoring algorithm. It exploits quantum mechanical properties to find prime factors in polynomial time — meaning the difficulty no longer scales exponentially with key size. The security foundation evaporates.

⚠ The Maze Analogy A classical computer solving factorization is like exploring a maze one path at a time. A quantum computer uses superposition to explore many paths simultaneously, and entanglement to collapse on the solution. For most problems, this provides no advantage. But for problems with mathematical structure like factoring, it is catastrophically effective.

Why Lattices Are Different — The Haystack Analogy

RSA works like a combination lock where quantum computers have a master decoder. The structure of the problem gives quantum algorithms something to exploit: mathematical periodicity that Shor's can detect through quantum interference.

🌾 The Haystack The lattice shortest-vector problem is like a haystack where everything looks like hay. There's no exploitable mathematical structure for quantum algorithms to latch onto. You still have to search — and at 500+ dimensions, even quantum computers have no known shortcut. This is why NIST chose lattice-based algorithms as the foundation of post-quantum cryptography.
⚠ What This Means for AES Grover's algorithm gives a quadratic speedup on key search — effectively halving the bit-security. AES-128 becomes AES-64 security. AES-256 becomes AES-128 security. This is manageable: just double key lengths. You don't need to replace AES — but you do need to ensure the key exchange mechanism that delivers AES keys is quantum-resistant.

NIST's 2024 Response: The New Standards

After an 8-year competition with 82 initial candidates, NIST finalized three post-quantum standards in August 2024. These are not optional upgrades — every organization using public-key cryptography must eventually migrate.

FIPS 203

ML-KEM (Kyber)

Role: Key Encapsulation Mechanism. Replaces RSA and ECDH for establishing shared secrets over insecure channels.

You'll use this in: Lab 2 — implementing key generation, encapsulation, and decapsulation.

FIPS 204

ML-DSA (Dilithium)

Role: Digital Signature Algorithm. Replaces ECDSA and RSA-PSS for signing documents and code.

You'll use this in: Lab 3 — signing, verifying, and benchmarking against classical signatures.

FIPS 205

SLH-DSA (SPHINCS+)

Role: Hash-based signature scheme. Conservative security foundation; larger signatures but minimal mathematical assumptions.

Best for: Long-lived systems needing maximum assurance.

📅 The 2035 Mandate NIST IR 8547 calls for full migration by 2035. That's roughly 10 years — which sounds comfortable until you consider that major infrastructure migrations routinely take 8–12 years. The TLS 1.0 → 1.2 transition took nearly a decade. PQC migration is significantly more complex. Organizations that start in 2030 will not finish by 2035.

Phase 5: The Takeaway
Why this changes everything about how we encrypt today
🔓 The Core Lesson The RSA-encrypted message was successfully decrypted by the quantum attacker — years after it was sent. The ML-KEM message remained confidential. This is the fundamental argument for migrating today: any data encrypted with classical algorithms that must remain private past the quantum threat horizon is already at risk.
What RSA Gets Wrong

Security based on integer factorization. Quantum computers solve this in polynomial time using Shor's algorithm. Any archived RSA ciphertext becomes readable.

What ML-KEM Gets Right

Security based on lattice problems (Learning With Errors). No known quantum algorithm provides meaningful speedup. Data stays protected even against future quantum hardware.

⏰ The Timeline Math If data must stay secret for 30 years, and quantum computers might arrive in 10–20 years, then anything encrypted today with RSA has a window of vulnerability. You don't need to know exactly when Q-Day arrives — if the confidentiality lifespan exceeds the quantum threat estimate, migration is already urgent.
