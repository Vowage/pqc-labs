What You Are Actually Migrating

Post-quantum migration is not one project — it is eight or more simultaneous projects with different owners, timelines, and constraints. This table is what makes it an organizational challenge, not just a technical one.

Infrastructure Layer Current Algorithm Replacement Typical Timeline Difficulty
⚠ Why Healthcare Is Uniquely Hard Medical devices are regulated by the FDA. Firmware updates require 510(k) clearance or PMA approval — a process that takes 3–5 years per device class and costs hundreds of thousands of dollars. A 2008 MRI system cannot simply receive a software patch to upgrade its cryptography. This is the hard constraint that drives all other decisions in this lab.
The Hard Constraint
Systems that cannot be updated via software — old MRI, cardiac monitors, infusion pumps — require FDA-regulated hardware replacement. Budget: $800K–$2M per system. Lead time: 18–36 months. This cannot be accelerated by throwing money at it.
Quick Wins
TLS on public-facing web servers can be migrated in days. Certificate renewal is automated. Cloud services can switch KMS algorithms in hours. These migrations cost almost nothing and should happen immediately — they are the "low-hanging fruit" that boards understand and approve quickly.
📊 Historical Precedent: Why 2035 Is Optimistic The SHA-1 deprecation was announced in 2011 with a 2017 deadline. As of 2024, SHA-1 is still found in active production systems — 7 years after the deadline. The MD5 deprecation (2004) still has legacy deployments in 2025. Large-scale cryptographic migrations consistently take 2–3× longer than planned. Plan accordingly.

Exercise C · Legacy Device Decision

The 2008 MRI Systems Cannot Be Updated

MedCore has three MRI imaging systems from 2008 using RSA-1024 for DICOM image signing. They cannot receive cryptographic software updates. They process protected health images 24/7. Evaluate the three options below.

🚨 The Stakes RSA-1024 is already considered below NIST's minimum security threshold for new deployments (broken in 2009 via distributed computing for certain key sizes). A quantum computer would break it trivially. These systems are signing medical images used for diagnosis — a forged image signature could go undetected. This is not a theoretical risk.
Option Description Security Impact Feasibility / Cost Your Recommendation
A — Network Isolation Segment MRI systems onto an air-gapped VLAN with no external network access. Signatures verified only within the isolated network.
B — PQ Proxy Gateway Install a post-quantum-capable proxy between MRI systems and the network. The proxy re-signs outgoing DICOM data with ML-DSA before it leaves the isolated segment.
C — Hardware Replacement Procure new MRI systems with ML-DSA and ML-KEM support. FDA-cleared devices with FIPS 204/203 capability are entering the market 2026–2027.
💡 Model Answer Breakdown Option A alone is insufficient — isolation prevents new attacks but does not address already-harvested DICOM signatures stored in radiology archives from the past 15 years. Option B is the strongest near-term mitigation — the proxy adds quantum-resistant signing without touching the FDA-cleared device. It costs $50K–$150K vs. $1–2M for full replacement and can be deployed in months. Option C is the only permanent solution — but takes 18–36 months and costs $1–2M per unit. The recommended approach is B immediately + C on the normal capital replacement cycle (5–7 years). This is a real decision framework used in healthcare cybersecurity.

The Hard Questions That Don't Have Clean Answers

Three questions that close the PQC series — connecting technical implementation to organizational reality and professional ethics.

1. Based on historical patterns in large-scale cryptographic transitions (SHA-1, MD5, DES, RC4), what is the most likely outcome for the NIST 2035 migration deadline?
A
Most critical infrastructure will meet the 2035 deadline because NIST enforcement mechanisms create compliance pressure.
B
The transition will be complete by 2035 for new deployments, but legacy systems will lag by 3–5 years on average.
C
Historical precedent strongly suggests the 2035 deadline will not be met by a significant portion of critical infrastructure. SHA-1 deprecation (announced 2011, deadline 2017) had active deployments in 2024 — 7 years after its deadline. MD5 (deprecated 2004) still runs in production in 2025. Large organizations consistently underestimate migration complexity. The most likely outcome is a 2035 deadline with 30–50% of critical infrastructure still running classical-only algorithms, creating a period of elevated risk during the CRQC transition window.
D
The deadline will be extended to 2040+ because CRQC timelines have historically been overestimated.
2. MedCore's 2008 MRI systems cannot be cryptographically updated and handle protected health images used for diagnosis. No PQ proxy has yet been deployed. What is the organization's ethical obligation during this window?
A
None — the organization is operating within currently accepted security standards, and quantum computers do not yet exist at scale.
B
Disclose the vulnerability to patients so they can consent to having their data processed by potentially vulnerable systems.
C
Organizations operating systems that handle sensitive data under a known, foreseeable cryptographic vulnerability have an obligation to: (1) document the risk formally in their risk register, (2) implement compensating controls immediately (network isolation, proxy signing, access logging), (3) accelerate procurement of replacement hardware, and (4) disclose to regulators if the vulnerability creates material HIPAA risk. "The technology does not yet exist to exploit this" is not a defense against the obligation to mitigate known risks — particularly under harvest-now-decrypt-later attacks, where today's data is already being collected for future decryption.
D
Immediately shut down the MRI systems until they can be replaced — patient safety requires zero tolerance for cryptographic risk.
3. Across this five-lab series you implemented ML-KEM, ML-DSA, hybrid key exchange, and a full migration plan. Which of the following best describes the single most important lesson from the series?
A
Post-quantum algorithms are drop-in replacements for classical algorithms — migration is primarily a software update problem.
B
The primary challenge of PQC migration is performance — ML-KEM and ML-DSA are significantly slower than their classical counterparts.
C
Quantum computers will definitively break all current cryptography by 2030, creating an urgent deadline with no flexibility.
D
Post-quantum migration is simultaneously a technical implementation problem, an organizational change management problem, a regulatory compliance problem, and an ethical obligation — and the hardest part is not the cryptography. The algorithms are standardized, the libraries exist, and the performance is acceptable. The hard part is identifying every system that needs migration, prioritizing under resource constraints, managing legacy devices that cannot be updated, communicating risk to non-technical decision-makers, and sustaining organizational will over a multi-year program. Technical knowledge of ML-KEM and ML-DSA is necessary but not sufficient — you also need to be able to write the board email.
