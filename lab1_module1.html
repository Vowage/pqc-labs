SECTION 1 OF 6
Concept · Shor's Algorithm & Quantum Vulnerability

Why Quantum Computers Break RSA — And Why They Can't Break Everything

In 3–5 sentences: the core idea. No physics required.

💡 The Core Idea RSA is secure because multiplying two large prime numbers together is fast, but reversing the process — figuring out which primes were multiplied — takes classical computers an astronomically long time. Quantum computers exploit a phenomenon called quantum superposition and interference to find the periodicity in modular arithmetic, which effectively shortcuts the factoring problem down from exponential time to polynomial time. Lattice problems don't have this shortcut. The mathematical structure that makes lattice problems hard does not exhibit the kind of periodicity that Shor's algorithm exploits.
RSA — Built on Factoring

Hard problem: Given N = p × q, find p and q.
Classical: Sub-exponential time — secure at 2048 bits.
Quantum: Polynomial time via Shor's algorithm.

The security assumption is permanently broken.

ML-KEM — Built on Lattices

Hard problem: Learning With Errors (LWE) — find a secret vector given noisy linear equations.
Classical: Exponential time.
Quantum: No known speedup — still exponential.

Quantum computers provide no meaningful advantage.

⚠ The Key Insight The transition from "quantum computers don't exist yet" to "all past RSA data is readable" is instantaneous. There is no warning. No gradual degradation. One machine, one day, and every archived RSA ciphertext becomes a solved puzzle.

Shor's algorithm works in two phases. The first phase — quantum period finding — uses a quantum Fourier transform to find the period of a specific modular function that encodes the factoring problem. The second phase extracts the prime factors from that period using classical math. The quantum part is what makes it fast.

How Shor's Attack Unfolds
🎯
Step 1
Choose target RSA modulus N
⚛️
Step 2 (Quantum)
Quantum Fourier transform finds period r of f(x) = a^x mod N
🔢
Step 3 (Classical)
Compute GCD(a^(r/2) ± 1, N) to get prime factors
🔓
Result
Private key recovered. All ciphertexts decrypted.
📐 Why Lattices Are Different Lattice problems do not have exploitable periodicity. The hardness of Learning With Errors comes from noise and high-dimensional geometry — properties that quantum interference cannot amplify or cancel out. This is not a temporary gap; mathematicians have studied this for over 30 years without finding a quantum shortcut.

🔑 Read This Table Carefully Shor's algorithm only targets problems with mathematical periodicity — specifically factoring and discrete logarithm. Grover's algorithm applies to symmetric search but only provides a quadratic speedup, meaning AES-256 (already using 256-bit keys) remains secure. Lattice problems have no applicable quantum algorithm at all.

A common misconception is that quantum computers break all cryptography. They don't. Grover's algorithm provides a quadratic speedup for unstructured search, which means brute-forcing a symmetric key goes from O(2n) to O(2n/2). For AES-256 that means 2128 operations instead of 2256 — which is still completely infeasible.

AES-128 — Concern

Grover's effectively halves the key length to 64-bit security equivalent. For highly classified applications, this is considered insufficient. Upgrade to AES-256.

AES-256 — Fine

2128 quantum operations remain computationally infeasible. No migration required for the encryption layer itself — only the key exchange mechanism (RSA/ECDH) needs replacement.

✅ Practical Takeaway If your system uses RSA or ECC for key exchange but AES-256 for bulk encryption (which most TLS sessions do), the bulk encryption layer is fine. The vulnerability is in the handshake — specifically where the symmetric key was negotiated using RSA or ECDH. Replace the key exchange layer with ML-KEM.

Phase 4 — Retroactive Decryption
Data from years ago becomes readable today.

This is the part that feels abstract until it isn't: a patient's HIV diagnosis encrypted in 2024 is readable in 2031. A company's merger strategy document from 2026 is readable now. A government agent's identity, confirmed via 2025 communications, is now known to the adversary.

🔓 The Decryption Happens in the Past This is not a future attack — it is a retroactive one. The breach did not occur in 2031. It occurred in 2024 when the data was intercepted and stored. 2031 is just when it became readable. From the victim's perspective, data they thought was secure has been compromised for years without their knowledge.

Option A: "Quantum computers use Shor's algorithm to factor large integers in polynomial time, which compromises the computational hardness assumptions underlying RSA and ECC. We recommend migrating to ML-KEM and ML-DSA as specified in NIST FIPS 203 and 204 respectively to maintain cryptographic agility ahead of the anticipated quantum threat threshold."
Option B: "We should upgrade our security systems because quantum computers are very powerful and could break our encryption in the future. This is an important thing to do and we should budget for it at some point."
Option C: "Adversaries have been recording our encrypted communications for years, betting on the ability to decrypt them once sufficiently powerful computers arrive.
